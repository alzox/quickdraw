Automated Decision-Making Demo

I developed 2 pet scenarios to explore the ethical and technical reality of "automated decision-making" systems.

Pet Insurance

This demo models a simplified prior authorization system. A user submits structured information through a form-pet name, breed, height, and weight—which is then evaluated against a fixed set of coverage rules. The outcome is generated automatically without further human review.

The system first applies deterministic checks. Certain inputs are disqualifying by definition: names matching a pattern, breeds outside an allowed category, or measurements exceeding hard limits. Applications that pass these filters are scored using a predefined weighting scheme and compared against a threshold. The result is returned as an acceptance or a denial.

What the system returns is only the decision, not the reasoning process in full. While the rules are explicit in this demo, many real prior authorization systems do not disclose which criteria triggered a denial or how contributing factors were weighed. In practice, patients and providers often receive a rejection without detailed justification beyond a general category such as "medical necessity" or "coverage criteria not met."

Obtaining more information typically requires initiating an appeals process. This involves contacting an insurance department, requesting a review, and submitting additional documentation. Appeals can take weeks to resolve and often require time, administrative labor, and clinical resources that patients or providers must supply themselves. These costs are external to the automated system but are a direct consequence of how it is designed and deployed.

The purpose of the demo is to make this structure visible. Even in a trivial case, eligibility depends entirely on prior definitions, cutoff values, and scoring choices. Once "operationalized", these definitions function as gatekeeping mechanisms.

Friend or Foe^1

This demo presents a minimal "classifier" operating over free-form visual input. The user draws an object on a canvas, and the system assigns one of two labels: friend or foe. The classification rule is deliberately narrow. Only drawings recognized as "cats"---when it can even pick up the cat---are labeled as friends. Everything else is treated as a foe. The classifier does not attempt to explain its decision. It outputs only the label. There is no indication of which features mattered, how close a drawing was to the boundary, or how the definition of "cat" is operationalized within the model.

This example mirrors the structure of friend-or-foe identification systems used in "autonomous weapon" contexts. In such systems, "classification" collapses a complex environment into a binary outcome that directly triggers downstream action. The critical choice is not the speed or accuracy of classification, but the prior definition of what counts as a legitimate target. Once that definition is encoded, the system acts on it, regardless of context.

From a technical standpoint, this rigidity is also a failure mode. If the training data or representational assumptions do not align with real-world conditions, the system degrades quickly and often silently. Models trained on highly specific distributions struggle with tasks that humans would consider trivial, not because recognition is intrinsically hard, but because the world presented to the model does not match the world it was trained to see^2.

From a societal standpoint, the classifier's authority begins earlier than the moment of classification. Developers first decide what a "cat" is supposed to look like in the world: which shapes matter, which features are ignored, and which variations are treated as noise. That definition is then encoded into data, features, and labels before the system ever sees user input.

When the output label is upgraded from a neutral category to a descriptor like friend or enemy, this act of definition takes on normative force. The system does not discover friends or enemies; it enacts a worldview supplied by its designers. Once deployed, these judgments are enforced uniformly, even though the social and moral assumptions that produced them are no longer visible at the point of decision.

Self Governance

The demos in this project were constructed to question the claim that automated decisions are "objective". At a narrow level, this claim is true. The arithmetic is correct. The functions execute as written. Given the same inputs, the system will always return the same output. But objectivity at the level of computation obscures where judgment is exercised in the first place.

Developers decide what variables exist, how they are represented, and which differences matter. In the pet insurance demo, the system does not "discover" that height or weight are relevant. Those attributes are selected in advance and assigned specific roles in the decision. The scoring function encodes a view of risk, even if that view is expressed as a formula. In the "prior authorization" framing, the system does not evaluate medical necessity as such; it operationalizes a proxy defined elsewhere and enforces it consistently.

Self-governance begins by acknowledging this layer of authorship. Automated systems do not eliminate discretion; they relocate it upstream. Once definitions, thresholds, and labels are fixed, the system appears neutral because it no longer requires human judgment at the point of execution. This neutrality is procedural rather than substantive. The hard questions have already been settled, just not in a visible or contestable way.

The friend-or-foe and hand-drawn classifier demos make this especially clear. The model does not infer what a "friend" is. It learns what a "cat" looks like from labeled data, and the decision to equate that label with friendship is imposed externally. When errors occur, they are treated as model failures rather than as consequences of earlier definitional choices. The system cannot account for ambiguity because ambiguity was never part of its ontology.

Self-governance, then, is less about making models explain themselves and more about making those upstream choices subject to scrutiny. Who defines the categories? Who selects the training data? Who decides which errors are tolerable and which are not? Without mechanisms for questioning and revising these decisions, automated systems turn contingent judgments into fixed infrastructure.

The demos do not argue against automation. They argue against hiding governance inside technical design. If automated systems are to be used in domains with real consequences, self-governance requires developers and institutions to treat definitions, representations, and thresholds as sites of responsibility rather than as neutral implementation details.
